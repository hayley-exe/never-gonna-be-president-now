Early Life and Education

Woodrow Wilson was born on December 28, 1856, in Staunton, Virginia, to Joseph Ruggles Wilson and Jessie Janet Woodrow. His father was a founder of the Presbyterian Church in the Confederate States of America. Wilson earned a Ph.D. in history and political science from Johns Hopkins University and later graduated from Princeton University before attending the University of Virginia School of Law. Raised in the South, his religious upbringing and the impact of the Civil War deeply influenced his views. A scholar, professor, and author, Wilson's teaching and leadership experience shaped his future political career.

Political Alignment and Election

As a Democrat, campaigned on the platform of "New Freedom," which opposed Theodore Roosevelt's "New Nationalism." His focus was on labor reforms, anti-corruption measures, banking reform, progressive taxation, tariff reform, and trust-busting through antitrust laws. During the Progressive Era, the Republican Party split, and social reforms along with the rise of labor movements gained traction. Wilson faced strong competition in the 1912 election from Republican incumbent William Howard Taft, Progressive Party candidate Theodore Roosevelt, and Socialist Eugene V. Debs. Major issues included controlling corporate monopolies, addressing income inequality, reforming the banking system, and advancing social welfare. Wilson won with 435 electoral votes (out of 531) and approximately 42% of the popular vote.
