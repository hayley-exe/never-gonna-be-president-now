Woodrow Wilson

A controversial president and detestable individual.

Early Life and Education

Woodrow Wilson was born on December 28, 1856, in Staunton, Virginia, to Joseph Ruggles Wilson and Jessie Janet Woodrow. His father was a founder of the Presbyterian Church in the Confederate States of America. Wilson earned a Ph.D. in history and political science from Johns Hopkins University and later graduated from Princeton University before attending the University of Virginia School of Law. Raised in the South, his religious upbringing and the impact of the Civil War deeply influenced his views. A scholar, professor, and author, Wilson's teaching and leadership experience shaped his future political career.

Did you know? Woodrow Wilson was the only U.S. president to hold a Ph.D.!

Political Alignment and Election

As a Democrat, Wilson campaigned on the platform of "New Freedom," opposing Theodore Roosevelt's "New Nationalism." His focus was on labor reforms, anti-corruption measures, banking reform, progressive taxation, tariff reform, and trust-busting through antitrust laws. Wilson won the 1912 election despite strong competition, securing 42% of the popular vote and 435 electoral votes.

Presidency

Wilson is remembered for his leadership during World War I and his advocacy for the League of Nations, despite the Senate's failure to ratify U.S. membership. He implemented progressive reforms like the Federal Reserve Act, reshaping the banking system. However, his presidency was marred by policies such as support for racial segregation in federal agencies, reversing progress on civil rights for African Americans. His health declined in his final years due to a stroke, leaving him unable to govern effectively.

After Presidency

After leaving office, Wilson continued to advocate for the League of Nations, believing it could prevent future conflicts. Although the U.S. did not join, he remained a vocal supporter. He wrote memoirs and made occasional public appearances, advocating for peace and social progress. Wilson passed away on February 3, 1924, due to complications from the stroke he suffered in 1919, marking the end of a controversial legacy.
